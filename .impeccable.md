## Design Context

### Users
Mixed hiring managers — both technical (CTOs, engineering leads) and non-technical (recruiters, HR) across company sizes. They're scanning quickly, often comparing against a stack of other resumes. The CV must work for both ATS parsing and human reading.

### Brand Personality
**Confident & understated.** Quiet competence — the work speaks for itself. No flashiness, no overselling. Three words: *composed, credible, sharp.*

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Visual tone**: Modern tech resume — clean, generous whitespace, bold section structure
- **Current foundation**: Libertinus serif with Butterick-inspired small-caps headings is a strong base, but lean toward more modern spacing and hierarchy
- **Typography**: Prioritize scannability — clear visual hierarchy between role titles, companies, dates, and bullet points
- **Color**: Muted, professional palette (`#2C3E50` accent, `#4A6274` links, `#5A6A7A` secondary text). No bright colors.
- **Anti-references**: Avoid dense academic CVs, cluttered layouts, or anything that feels like a LaTeX default template

### Emotional Goals
The reader should feel:
1. **Respect & authority** — this person is senior and deeply knows their craft
2. **Excitement & curiosity** — I want to talk to this person
3. **Calm confidence** — no overselling, just substance

### Design Principles
1. **Scannability first** — a hiring manager should grasp seniority level, domain, and impact in 6 seconds
2. **Let whitespace do the work** — generous spacing signals confidence; cramming signals desperation
3. **Hierarchy over decoration** — use weight, size, and spacing to guide the eye, not lines or boxes
4. **Every word earns its place** — if a bullet point doesn't demonstrate impact or scope, cut it
5. **ATS-friendly always** — real text, standard fonts, no tables or graphics that break parsing
